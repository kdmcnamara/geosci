Scientific / Engineering Challenges:

Technical challenges with the process of recycling are not hard to come by, after the materials arrive at the recycling facility numerous technological challenges take place before it can be shipped out as raw material. Most of the challenges stem from sorting the materials out effetely. As there is profits to be made in recycling, many innovations have solved numerous challenges. But as the industry is privatized, each facility is not identical, and thus not all locations have the same capabilities. According to a study by the Environmental Protection Agency, the United States generated about 258 million tons of municipal solid waste in 2014. However, 34.6% of municipal solid waste (MSW) was recovered through recycling (EPA, n.d.). MSW contains materials such as plastic, steel, tires, glass, foam, and other non-biodegradable products, which have been mass-produced over the past decades. These materials can persist in the environment for centuries if not millennia due to their durability, ultimately causing waste-management issues and environmental damage.

One such scenario that makes automation so challenging is sorting Low-density polyethylene (LDPE) from Polypropylene (PP). The two plastics have nearly identical densities, are used frequently and make up nearly 50% of the U.S.’s plastic waste (Vanderbilt, 2004). Plastic film, such as plastic bags, is another challenge for the recycling process. Due to the flexibility of this material, they can wreak havoc on the sorting cycle and damage equipment. Therefore though the material itself is recyclable, for municipal use plastic films are considered a non-recyclable material. The fallowing video and article illustrate the issue in action.
